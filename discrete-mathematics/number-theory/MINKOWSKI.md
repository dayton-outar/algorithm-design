# Minkowski’s Theorem and Convex Regions

Let ${P}$ and ${Q}$ be points of ${\Lambda}$, and let ${P'}$ and ${Q'}$ be their reflections about the origin. Add to the parallelogram ${J}$ (from [Farey Dissection](./FAREY.md#farey-dissection)) the three parallelograms based on ${OQ, OP}$; ${OP, OQ'}$; and ${OQ', OP}$. This construction produces a larger parallelogram ${K}$ centered at the origin with area ${4\delta}$, four times the area of ${J}$. If ${\delta = 1}$ (its minimum value), lattice points may lie on the boundary of ${K}$ but none lie strictly inside except the origin.

If ${\delta > 1}$, then ${K}$ contains lattice points other than the origin.

This result is a special case of Minkowski’s theorem: any region symmetric about the origin behaves this way, not just parallelograms. 

## Open, Closed, and Convex Regions

An **open region** ${R}$ has two properties:

* If a point ${P}$ lies in ${R}$, then all nearby points also lie in ${R}$
* Any two points in ${R}$ can be joined by a continuous curve that stays entirely inside ${R}$

A point inside ${R}$ is called an interior point. The boundary consists of points that limit ${R}$ but are not inside it.

A **closed region** includes both ${R}$ and its boundary.

### Convexity

A region ${R}$ is **convex** if every line segment joining two points in ${R}$ lies entirely inside ${R}$.

Equivalently:

* For any two points ${P, Q \in R}$, the entire segment ${PQ}$ lies in ${R}$
* Or: for any point ${P \in R}$, there exists a line through ${P}$ such that the whole region lies on one side of that line

Circles and parallelograms are convex.

Convexity does not change under translation or scaling.

## Minkowski’s Theorem

If a convex region ${R}$:

* is symmetric about the origin
* has area greater than ${4}$

then ${R}$ contains a lattice point other than the origin.

## Strategy of the Proof

We first prove a simpler result.

### Auxiliary Result

Let ${R_0}$ be an open region containing the origin. Construct translated copies ${R_P}$ centered at each lattice point ${P}$.

If no two such regions overlap, then the area of ${R_0}$ is at most ${1}$.

Sketch of idea:

* Count how many translated regions fit inside a large square
* Compare total area covered vs available space
* Let the square grow and take limits

This forces
${\Delta \le 1}$

## Main Proof Idea

Assume a convex symmetric region ${R}$ has area greater than ${4}$.

Scale it down to a region ${R_0}$ with area greater than ${1}$. Then:

* By the auxiliary result, translated copies must overlap
* So there exist two lattice points whose corresponding regions intersect

Using symmetry and convexity, this implies:

* A midpoint of two lattice points lies in ${R}$
* That midpoint is also a lattice point

Hence ${R}$ contains a non-zero lattice point.

## Alternative Argument (Counting Method)

Represent lattice points as
${x = \frac{2p}{t},\quad y = \frac{2q}{t}}$

Divide the plane into small squares and count how many lie inside ${R}$.

As ${t \to \infty}$:

* The count approaches the area of ${R}$
* If area ${> 4}$, there must be enough points to force two with the same remainder pattern

Their difference gives a lattice point inside ${R}$.

## Fundamental Lattice and Area

Let ${L}$ be a lattice generated by two vectors.

The area of its fundamental parallelogram is
${\delta = OP \cdot OQ \cdot \sin \omega}$

A linear transformation
${x' = \alpha x + \beta y,\quad y' = \gamma x + \delta y}$
scales all areas by
${|\alpha\delta - \beta\gamma|}$

## General Form of Minkowski’s Theorem

Let ${\Lambda}$ be any lattice with fundamental area ${\delta}$.

If a region ${R_0}$:

* is symmetric about the origin
* has area greater than ${\delta}$

then it contains a non-zero lattice point.

## Equivalence of Points

Two points ${(x,y)}$ and ${(x',y')}$ are equivalent if
${x' - x = r x_1 + s x_2,\quad y' - y = r y_1 + s y_2}$
for integers ${r,s}$.

## Final Result

If a region has:

* area equal to the fundamental parallelogram
* no two equivalent points inside it

then every point in the plane is equivalent to some point in that region.

If this were false, a contradiction arises from comparing areas.

## Higher Dimensions

The argument extends to higher dimensions:

* Replace area with volume
* Replace ${4}$ with ${2^n}$

So in ${n}$ dimensions, any symmetric convex region with volume greater than ${2^n}$ times the fundamental volume contains a non-zero lattice point.
