# Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic in Quadratic Integer Rings

## Algebraic Integers

Algebraic number systems extend the rational integers by adjoining roots such as ${i}$ or ${\omega}$. Their elements behave like numbers, but divisibility can change dramatically.

An algebraic integer is a root of a monic polynomial with integer coefficients. In the Gaussian integers, the typical element has the form

${a+bi}$

with ${a,b\in\mathbb Z}$. In Eisenstein-type rings, elements have the form

${a+b\omega}$

where ${\omega}$ is a primitive cube root of unity.

## Norms and Units

A norm converts divisibility questions into ordinary integer size. For Gaussian integers,

${N(a+bi)=a^2+b^2}$

The only Gaussian units are

${\pm1,\ \pm i}$

Units divide every element and must be ignored when discussing unique factorization.

## Euclidean Algorithm

The Gaussian integers support a Euclidean algorithm because every complex number lies close to a Gaussian integer. If ${\alpha}$ and ${\beta\ne0}$ are Gaussian integers, choose ${q}$ close to ${\alpha/\beta}$ and write

${\alpha=q\beta+r}$

with ${N(r)<N(\beta)}$. Repeating this division produces a greatest common divisor.

## Unique Factorization

Once a Euclidean algorithm exists, the usual proof of the fundamental theorem of arithmetic works. Every non-zero non-unit factors into primes, and the factorization is unique up to order and multiplication by units.

In the Gaussian integers this gives a complete analogue of ordinary prime factorization.

## Gaussian Primes

Rational primes behave in three ways inside ${\mathbb Z[i]}$:

* ${2}$ ramifies, since ${2=-(1+i)^2 i}$ up to a unit.
* A prime ${p\equiv1\pmod 4}$ splits as ${p=(a+bi)(a-bi)}$.
* A prime ${p\equiv3\pmod 4}$ remains prime in ${\mathbb Z[i]}$.

This classification links prime factorization to representation by two squares.

## Eisenstein Integers

The ring generated by a cube root of unity has a similar geometry and a similar Euclidean algorithm. Its norm supports division with remainder, prime factorization, and a classification of rational primes according to congruence classes modulo ${3}$.

These examples show how arithmetic can remain familiar in special algebraic integer rings while already hinting at the failures that appear in more general fields.
