# The Series of Primes

## Chebyshev Functions

The functions

${\theta(x)=\sum_{p\le x}\log p}$

and

${\psi(x)=\sum_{p^k\le x}\log p}$

encode the distribution of primes in weighted form. Showing that these functions have order ${x}$ gives strong evidence for the prime number theorem.

## Bertrand's Postulate

Bertrand's postulate states that for every ${n>1}$ there is a prime between ${n}$ and ${2n}$. Chebyshev-type estimates for factorials and binomial coefficients prove this interval always contains a prime.

## Sums and Products over Primes

The divergence of

${\sum_p \frac1p}$

shows that primes are sparse but not too sparse. Related products such as

${\prod_{p\le x}\left(1-\frac1p\right)}$

measure the density of integers avoiding small prime factors.

## Mertens-Type Results

Mertens's theorem gives asymptotic control over prime sums and products. These estimates serve as elementary substitutes for deeper analytic prime number results.

## Number of Prime Factors

Let ${\omega(n)}$ count distinct prime factors and ${\Omega(n)}$ count prime factors with multiplicity. Their normal order is

${\log\log n}$

This means most integers near ${n}$ have about ${\log\log n}$ prime factors, even though exceptional integers may have many more or fewer.

## Normal Order of Divisors

The divisor function ${d(n)}$ has normal order much smaller than its largest values. Prime-factor statistics explain the typical size of ${d(n)}$.

## Selberg's Theorem and Prime Intervals

Selberg's elementary method supplies powerful estimates for primes without complex analysis. The chapter uses these ideas to approach prime distribution in intervals and products of prime factors.

## Prime Pair Conjectures

The chapter closes with conjectures about prime pairs, especially pairs

${p,\ p+2}$

The twin prime problem remains a prototype for questions about fine-scale prime distribution.
