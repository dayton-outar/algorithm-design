# Representation by Two or Four Squares

## Waring's Problem

Waring's problem asks whether every positive integer can be written as a bounded number of ${k}$th powers. The functions ${g(k)}$ and ${G(k)}$ measure how many powers are needed in exact and asymptotic forms.

For squares, the decisive results are especially strong.

## Two Squares

An integer is representable as a sum of two squares exactly when every prime ${p\equiv3\pmod4}$ occurs to an even power in its prime factorization.

For primes, Fermat's theorem says

${p=a^2+b^2}$

exactly when ${p=2}$ or ${p\equiv1\pmod4}$.

## Four Squares

Lagrange's four-square theorem states that every positive integer is a sum of four integer squares:

${n=a^2+b^2+c^2+d^2}$

This proves ${g(2)=4}$.

## Quaternions

Quaternions provide an algebraic proof of the four-square theorem. A quaternion has the form

${a+bi+cj+dk}$

with multiplication rules

${i^2=j^2=k^2=ijk=-1}$

The norm

${N(a+bi+cj+dk)=a^2+b^2+c^2+d^2}$

is multiplicative, which makes four-square identities natural.

## Integral Quaternions

Integral quaternion arithmetic supplies a division-like theory and a highest common right-hand divisor. This supports a prime-factor argument for four-square representation.

## More Squares

Representations by larger numbers of squares become increasingly regular. Generating functions and theta identities count the number of representations and connect additive number theory with modular forms.
