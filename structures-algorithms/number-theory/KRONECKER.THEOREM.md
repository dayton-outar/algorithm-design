# Kronecker's Theorem

## One-Dimensional Approximation

Kronecker's theorem extends rational approximation from single numbers to linear forms. In one dimension, it describes when multiples of a real number come arbitrarily close to prescribed targets modulo ${1}$.

If ${\alpha}$ is irrational, the fractional parts of

${n\alpha}$

come arbitrarily close to every point in the unit interval.

## Proof Ideas

The one-dimensional proof again relies on pigeonhole reasoning and the density of fractional parts. The method partitions the unit interval, finds small steps, and walks by those steps to approach a target.

## Reflected Ray Problem

The reflected ray problem gives a geometric model. A ray reflecting inside a rectangle traces positions whose coordinates reduce modulo side lengths. Kronecker's theorem predicts when the ray comes close to a chosen point.

## General Theorem

The multidimensional form concerns simultaneous approximations to linear expressions

${n_1\alpha_1+n_2\alpha_2+\cdots+n_k\alpha_k}$

modulo ${1}$. The only obstructions come from integer linear relations among the coefficients.

## Equivalent Forms

The theorem can be stated either as an approximation result or as a density result on a torus. These forms are equivalent: approximation to every target means the corresponding set of points is dense.

## Uniform Distribution

Density says that points come arbitrarily close to every region. Uniform distribution says they visit regions with the correct limiting frequency.

Kronecker's theorem prepares the ground for uniform distribution by explaining when arithmetic progressions on a torus fill space rather than closing into a smaller cycle.
