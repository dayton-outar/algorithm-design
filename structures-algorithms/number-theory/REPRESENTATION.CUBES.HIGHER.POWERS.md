# Representation by Cubes and Higher Powers

## Higher Waring Problems

For ${k\ge3}$, Waring's problem becomes harder. One asks for bounds on ${g(k)}$, the number of ${k}$th powers needed to represent every positive integer, and ${G(k)}$, the number needed for all sufficiently large integers.

## Biquadrates and Cubes

Identities show that enough fourth powers or cubes can represent all integers after suitable reductions. The proofs build upper bounds by transforming large powers into controlled sums of smaller ones.

For cubes, the existence of finite bounds is already nontrivial. The chapter develops explicit estimates rather than merely proving existence.

## Lower Bounds

Lower bounds come from congruences and size constraints. Some residue classes cannot be reached with too few powers, so ${g(k)}$ and ${G(k)}$ must exceed specific thresholds.

For powers of ${2}$, numbers just below a large power often force many summands.

## Signed Sums

Allowing signs changes the problem. A signed representation has the form

${n=\pm x_1^k\pm x_2^k\pm\cdots\pm x_s^k}$

The function ${v(k)}$ measures how many signed powers suffice. Signs often reduce the number of terms needed.

## Prouhet-Tarry Problems

The Prouhet-Tarry problem asks for different sets of integers whose sums of like powers agree:

${\sum_i a_i^r=\sum_i b_i^r}$

for several exponents ${r}$. Such identities control cancellation among powers and feed into Waring-type estimates.

## Further Diophantine Problems

The chapter ends with related equations and bounds. The common theme is that additive representation by high powers depends on a blend of identities, congruences, descent, and estimates.
