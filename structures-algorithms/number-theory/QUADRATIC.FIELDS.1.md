# Quadratic Fields

## Algebraic Fields

A quadratic field has the form

${\mathbb Q(\sqrt m)}$

where ${m}$ is a squarefree integer. Its elements look like

${a+b\sqrt m}$

with ${a,b\in\mathbb Q}$. The arithmetic of such fields extends ordinary rational arithmetic while introducing new integers, units, primes, and factorization behavior.

## Algebraic Integers

The algebraic integers of a quadratic field are the roots of monic quadratic equations with integer coefficients. Their exact form depends on ${m\pmod 4}$:

${a+b\sqrt m}$

or

${\frac{a+b\sqrt m}{2}}$

with suitable parity conditions.

## Units and Primes

A unit is an algebraic integer with an algebraic integer inverse. In norm language, units have norm ${\pm1}$.

For real quadratic fields, the unit group is often infinite. In ${\mathbb Q(\sqrt2)}$, powers of

${1+\sqrt2}$

generate infinitely many units and connect the field to Pell-type equations.

## Failure of Unique Factorization

Unique factorization does not hold in every quadratic field. The same element may factor into irreducibles in inequivalent ways. This failure shows that Euclidean division is a special property, not a general feature of algebraic integers.

## Euclidean Quadratic Fields

Some quadratic fields still support a Euclidean algorithm through a norm. Complex Euclidean fields resemble the Gaussian and Eisenstein cases. Real Euclidean fields are subtler because their geometry is unbounded in one direction.

When a Euclidean algorithm exists, greatest common divisors and unique factorization follow. When it fails, arithmetic needs stronger tools.
