# Some Diophantine Equations

## Fermat-Type Equations

Diophantine equations ask for integer or rational solutions. Fermat's equation

${x^n+y^n=z^n}$

has abundant structure even before the full theorem is available.

The equation for ${n=2}$ has infinitely many primitive solutions. Every primitive Pythagorean triple has the form

${x=m^2-n^2,\quad y=2mn,\quad z=m^2+n^2}$

after swapping ${x}$ and ${y}$ if needed, with ${m}$ and ${n}$ coprime and of opposite parity.

## Fourth Powers

The equation

${x^4+y^4=z^2}$

has no non-zero integer solution. Infinite descent proves this: a minimal solution would produce a smaller one, which is impossible.

This result implies the Fermat equation for exponent ${4}$:

${x^4+y^4=z^4}$

## Cubic Equations

The chapter also studies cubic forms such as

${x^3+y^3=z^3}$

and related variants. These equations require more delicate factorization, often in algebraic integer rings where expressions such as ${x^3+y^3}$ split.

## Rational Sums of Cubes

Some equations that fail over integers become flexible over rationals. A rational number may sometimes be expressed as a sum of rational cubes by transforming the equation into a problem about rational points.

This marks a shift from pure divisibility arguments toward the geometry of rational solutions.

## Several Variables

Equations with more variables, such as sums of several powers, behave differently from two-term Fermat equations. Extra variables create room for solutions, identities, and parametrizations.

The central lesson is that the arithmetic of powers depends not only on the exponent but also on the number of variables and the ring in which factorization occurs.
