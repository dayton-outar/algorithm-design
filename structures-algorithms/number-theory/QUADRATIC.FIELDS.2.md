# Quadratic Fields: Primes and Ideals

## Primes in ${\mathbb Z[i]}$

Gaussian prime factorization classifies ordinary primes by congruence:

${p=2}$

ramifies, primes ${p\equiv1\pmod4}$ split, and primes ${p\equiv3\pmod4}$ remain prime.

This classification also proves Fermat's two-square theorem: an odd prime is a sum of two squares exactly when it is congruent to ${1}$ modulo ${4}$.

## Primes in Eisenstein Integers

In the Eisenstein integers, rational primes split or remain prime according to congruence modulo ${3}$. This arithmetic supports results about representations by quadratic forms and about cubic residues.

## Other Quadratic Fields

Fields such as ${\mathbb Q(\sqrt2)}$ and ${\mathbb Q(\sqrt5)}$ show similar but distinct splitting behavior. The norm equation determines whether a rational prime factors in the ring of integers.

## Lucas's Test

Lucas's test studies Mersenne numbers

${M_p=2^p-1}$

through recurrence sequences. The test gives a practical primality criterion for special exponents and illustrates how algebraic number theory can produce computational tests.

## Ideals

Ideals repair the failure of unique factorization for elements. Even when elements do not factor uniquely, ideals can factor uniquely into prime ideals.

An ideal behaves like a generalized divisor. Replacing element factorization with ideal factorization restores a clean arithmetic structure in many quadratic fields.

## Wider Algebraic Fields

The same themes continue in higher-degree fields: norms, units, prime splitting, Euclidean algorithms, and ideals. Quadratic fields provide the first serious model for these ideas because they are concrete enough to compute with but rich enough to reveal the main difficulties.
